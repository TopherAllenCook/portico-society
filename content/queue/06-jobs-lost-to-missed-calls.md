Most home service businesses lose more jobs to missed calls than to any competitor. Every call that goes to voicemail after hours, during a job, or at a busy moment is a homeowner who will usually just call the next company on the list. The jobs do not show up as lost because you never knew they existed, which is exactly what makes missed calls so expensive.

## TL;DR: Key Takeaways

- A missed call is rarely a missed call. It is a job that called your competitor next.
- Most homeowners will not leave a voicemail. They hang up and dial the next contractor.
- The cost is invisible: you never see the jobs you lost, so the leak goes unnoticed.
- Around 80 percent of after-hours calls go to the company that answers, not the one the homeowner meant to call.
- The fix is a system: an after-hours answer, instant missed-call text back, and fast follow-up.

## Why are missed calls such a big problem for contractors?

The trouble with a missed call is that it does not feel like a loss. The phone rings, no one is free to answer, and the moment passes. There is no record of the job you just lost, no angry customer, no obvious cost. The homeowner on the other end simply moves on to the next name on their list, and you never learn that the opportunity was there.

That invisibility is the whole problem. A contractor can be excellent at the work, spend real money on marketing, and still quietly bleed jobs through unanswered calls without ever seeing it on a report. The leads arrive, the phone rings, and a meaningful share of those rings go unanswered and uncounted. Over a month, the lost revenue is often far larger than owners expect, precisely because nothing forces them to notice it.

## Why do homeowners not leave a voicemail?

Because they do not have to. A homeowner with a problem has a list of contractors and a phone, and calling the next one takes ten seconds. Leaving a voicemail and waiting for a callback feels slower and less certain, especially when the problem is urgent. So most people skip the voicemail entirely and dial the next number, and whoever picks up first usually gets the job.

This is why "we'll see the voicemail and call them back" is not a plan. Most of the calls you miss leave no voicemail at all. The homeowner is not annoyed, they are just gone, and they have already found someone else by the time you would have called back. The job was decided in the few minutes after your phone went unanswered.

## When do contractors miss the most calls?

Calls get missed at the worst possible times, which are usually the times homeowners most need help. After hours, when no one is in the office, an emergency call goes straight to voicemail and straight to a competitor. During the workday, calls come in while crews are on a job, hands are full, and no one can pick up. On busy days, the phone rings while the office is already on another call.

The pattern is that missed calls cluster exactly when demand is highest. A heat wave, a cold snap, a storm, these are the moments when the phone rings most and when your team is most stretched, so more calls go unanswered just as more jobs are on the line. Without a system to catch those calls, your busiest, most valuable days are also your leakiest.

## How much do missed calls actually cost?

The math is sobering once you put numbers to it. Take your average job value and multiply it by even a handful of missed calls a week, and the annual figure is often tens of thousands of dollars in lost revenue. For higher-ticket trades, a single missed call can be a multi-thousand-dollar install that went to someone else.

Here is a simple way to estimate your own exposure: count the calls you miss in a typical week, estimate how many would have booked, and multiply by your average job value. Most owners who run that math for the first time are shocked, because the number is real revenue that never appeared on any report. ## How do you stop losing jobs to missed calls?

You stop the leak by making sure every call gets a response, even when no one can pick up the phone. The goal is that a homeowner who calls always gets something fast, a real answer, a text back, or a clear way to book, rather than silence.

- **Answer after hours.** An AI call agent or answering service makes sure calls outside business hours reach a real, helpful response instead of voicemail.
- **Text back missed calls instantly.** An automatic text within seconds of a missed call keeps the homeowner engaged and offers a way to book before they dial the next company.
- **Follow up fast.** Quick, automated follow-up keeps unbooked callers moving toward an estimate.
- **Make booking easy.** A simple scheduling link turns a recovered call into a booked job without phone tag.

This is the same principle as [speed to lead](https://www.vervemd.com/blog/speed-to-lead-contractors): the first credible response usually wins, so the worst outcome is no response at all.

## What mistakes do contractors make with missed calls?

- **Assuming missed callers will leave a voicemail.** Most will not, and most will not call back either.
- **Relying on one person to answer everything.** When that person is busy, the calls and the jobs slip.
- **Ignoring after-hours and weekend calls.** Emergencies do not wait for business hours, and neither do the homeowners making them.
- **Never measuring missed calls.** If you do not track how many calls go unanswered, you cannot see the leak or fix it.

## Why don't I notice the jobs I'm losing to missed calls?

The hardest part of the missed-call problem is that it leaves no trace. When you win a job, you see it on the calendar. When you lose a bid, you usually know you were in the running. But when a call goes unanswered and the homeowner books someone else, nothing tells you it happened. There is no notification, no follow-up, no record. The job simply never enters your world.

That silence is why so many otherwise sharp owners underestimate the problem. They look at their booked jobs and their empty voicemail and conclude that missed calls are rare, because the evidence of the lost ones is invisible by definition. The only way to see the leak is to measure the calls themselves, how many come in, how many are answered, and how many go unanswered, rather than judging by the jobs that happened to land. Once you look at the calls instead of the bookings, the gap usually becomes obvious, and uncomfortable.

## Do missed calls hurt more than just one lost job?

Yes, and this is the part owners often miss. A missed call does not just cost the one job. It can cost the lifetime value of that customer and everyone they would have referred. A homeowner who needed an emergency repair today might have become a maintenance customer for years and a steady source of referrals. When that call goes to a competitor, you do not lose a single transaction, you lose the whole relationship and everything that would have flowed from it.

There is a reputational angle too. A homeowner who cannot reach you may form an impression that you are hard to get hold of, and impressions travel. In a tight-knit local market, being known as the company that does not answer is a quiet but real drag on every other marketing dollar you spend. The flip side is the opportunity: being the company that always answers becomes a reputation that compounds in your favor, one captured call at a time.

## Frequently asked questions

### How many calls do home service businesses typically miss?

It varies, but many contractors miss a meaningful share of inbound calls, especially after hours, during jobs, and on busy days. The exact number is less important than the fact that most missed calls are never recovered, because homeowners rarely leave voicemails and usually book whoever answers next.

### Do most people leave a voicemail if I miss their call?

No. Most homeowners will not leave a voicemail. Calling the next contractor is faster and feels more certain, especially for urgent work, so the majority of missed calls simply move on to a competitor without leaving any message behind.

### What is a missed-call text back?

It is an automatic text message sent within seconds of a missed call, letting the homeowner know you got their call and offering a quick way to book or get help. It keeps the lead warm and gives them a reason to wait for you instead of dialing the next company.

### How much revenue am I losing to missed calls?

Estimate it by multiplying the calls you miss each week by your booking rate and your average job value. For most contractors the annual figure runs into the tens of thousands of dollars, and for high-ticket trades a single missed install can be worth thousands on its own.

### Should I use an answering service or an AI call agent?

Either can work, and the right choice depends on your volume and budget. The point is that calls get answered fast and credibly at any hour, rather than going to voicemail. An AI call agent can answer around the clock, qualify the lead, and book the estimate, often at a lower cost than staffing the phones overnight.

### Are after-hours calls really worth answering?

Yes. Around 80 percent of after-hours calls go to the company that answers, not the one the homeowner intended to call. Emergencies are often the highest-value, most urgent jobs, so answering after hours captures work that competitors with voicemail are giving away.
