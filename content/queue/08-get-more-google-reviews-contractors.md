The fastest way to get more Google reviews is to ask every satisfied customer right after the job, make it effortless with a direct link, and respond to every review you receive. Reviews are one of the highest-return assets a contractor can build, because they drive local ranking, win the homeowner's trust at the moment of decision, and feed the AI assistants that now recommend businesses by name.

- Ask every happy customer for a review right after the job, while the experience is fresh.
- Make it effortless: send a direct review link by text, not a vague "find us on Google."
- Respond to every review, positive or negative, because it builds trust and signals an active business.
- Recent, steady reviews matter more than a one-time burst, so build a repeatable habit.
- Reviews feed local ranking, conversion, and AI recommendations all at once, so the effort pays off three ways.

## Why do Google reviews matter so much for contractors?

Reviews do three jobs at once, which is why they punch above their weight. First, they drive your local ranking and your place in the Google map pack, where a large share of homeowners find contractors. Second, they win trust at the exact moment a homeowner is deciding who to call, since most people read reviews before they pick a service business. Third, and increasingly, they feed the AI assistants that recommend contractors by name, because a steady stream of recent, positive reviews signals a trustworthy, active business.

That triple effect makes reviews one of the best returns on effort available to a contractor. They cost nothing but attention, they compound over time, and they influence both whether a homeowner finds you and whether they choose you. A business with a healthy, recent review profile has an advantage that is hard for a competitor to buy, because it has to be earned one job at a time.

## How do you get more Google reviews?

The core of it is simple: ask, make it easy, and do it consistently. Most satisfied customers are happy to leave a review, they just never get asked, or they get asked in a way that is too much work. Close those two gaps and your review count climbs steadily.

Start by asking every satisfied customer right after the job is done, while the experience and the relief of a solved problem are fresh. The longer you wait, the less likely they are to follow through. Then make it effortless. Send a direct link to your Google review page by text message, so leaving a review takes a few taps rather than a hunt through Google. A specific, friendly ask with a one-tap link will always outperform a generic "leave us a review online."

Most important, make it a habit rather than a campaign. A burst of reviews followed by months of silence looks worse to both Google and homeowners than a steady trickle. Build the ask into your normal close-out process so every completed job becomes a chance for a review, and the flow becomes self-sustaining.

## How should you ask for a Google review?

The best ask is personal, immediate, and easy to act on. Right after you finish the work and the customer is happy, let them know that reviews really help your business and that you would be grateful if they shared their experience. Keep it warm and human, not scripted. Then follow up with a text that contains a direct link to your Google review page, so the moment of goodwill turns into an actual review before the day moves on.

Timing and ease do most of the work. A homeowner who just watched you fix their problem is at their most willing, and a one-tap link removes the friction that kills good intentions. If you wait a week and send a vague email asking them to find you on Google, most will never get around to it. Ask at the peak of satisfaction, and make saying yes take seconds.

## Should you respond to Google reviews?

Yes, and it matters more than most contractors realize. Responding to reviews, both positive and negative, signals to Google and to future readers that you are an active, engaged business that cares about its customers. A simple, genuine thank-you on a positive review reinforces the goodwill. A calm, professional reply to a critical one often impresses future readers more than the perfect ratings around it, because it shows how you handle a problem.

The way you respond to a hard review is especially powerful. Future customers read it closely, and a measured, solution-focused reply tells them that if anything ever goes wrong, you will handle it like a professional. Defensiveness does the opposite. Treat every review, good or bad, as a chance to show prospective customers who you are.

## What should you avoid when getting reviews?

There are a few practices that backfire, and they are worth naming. Do not buy fake reviews. They violate Google's policies, they read as fake to homeowners, and they put your profile at risk. Do not gate reviews by only asking happy customers through a filter that screens out unhappy ones, because that practice is against the rules and erodes the trust reviews are supposed to build. And do not offer payment or discounts in exchange for reviews, which also violates policy.

The honest path is also the durable one. Earn reviews by doing good work and asking for them, respond to what you get, and let the profile reflect reality. A genuine, recent, well-tended review profile beats a manipulated one every time, because both homeowners and the systems ranking you are increasingly good at telling the difference.

## How do Google reviews affect AI search?

AI assistants lean on reviews as a trust signal when they decide which businesses to name. When a homeowner asks an assistant for the best contractor in their area, a business with a steady stream of recent, positive, responded-to reviews looks active and dependable, which makes the assistant more comfortable recommending it. A profile with a few old reviews and no responses signals the opposite.

## Frequently asked questions

### How do I get customers to actually leave a review?

Ask every satisfied customer right after the job, tell them it genuinely helps your business, and send a direct link by text so it takes a few taps. The combination of good timing and zero friction is what turns willing customers into actual reviews. Most people are happy to help, they just need to be asked and made it easy.

### When is the best time to ask for a Google review?

Right after you complete the work, while the customer is still feeling the relief of a solved problem. That is the peak of goodwill, and the longer you wait the less likely they are to follow through. Asking on the spot and following up with a link the same day works far better than waiting days.

### Should I respond to negative reviews?

Yes. A calm, professional, solution-focused response to a negative review often impresses future readers more than your positive reviews do, because it shows how you handle a problem. Defensiveness hurts you. Treat a hard review as a chance to demonstrate, in public, that you take care of your customers.

### Is it okay to offer a discount for a review?

No. Offering payment or discounts in exchange for reviews violates Google's policies and undermines the trust reviews are meant to build. Earn reviews by doing good work and asking for them, not by paying for them. Genuine reviews are both compliant and more convincing.

### How many Google reviews do I need?

There is no magic number, and recency matters as much as volume. A steady stream of recent reviews signals an active business, while a large pile of old ones can look stale. Focus on building a consistent habit so new reviews keep arriving, rather than chasing a single target and then stopping.

### Can I ask for reviews by text message?

Yes, and it is usually the most effective way. A text with a direct link to your Google review page is easy to act on in the moment, and most people read texts far faster than email. Send it right after the job, keep it short and friendly, and make the link the only thing they need to tap.

### Do Google reviews help me show up in AI search?

Yes, indirectly. AI assistants treat a steady flow of recent, positive, responded-to reviews as a trust signal, which makes them more likely to name your business when a homeowner asks for a recommendation. Reviews feed your local ranking and your AI visibility at the same time.
