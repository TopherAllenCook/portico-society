If your Google Ads aren't booking jobs, the problem is rarely the clicks. It is what happens after the click: weak tracking that hides which ads work, clicks sent to your homepage instead of a focused landing page, and slow follow-up on the leads you do get. Fix the path from click to booked job and the spend starts paying back.

## TL;DR: Key Takeaways

- Most failing Google Ads campaigns have a conversion problem, not a click problem.
- The usual causes: no call tracking, clicks landing on the homepage, broad keywords, no negative keywords, and slow lead response.
- Optimize for cost per booked job, not cost per click or even cost per lead.
- The average home services cost per click is about 7.85 dollars ([LocalIQ, 2025](https://localiq.com/blog/home-services-search-advertising-benchmarks/)), so wasted clicks add up fast.
- Small fixes to tracking and landing pages often recover more than raising the budget ever would.

## Why are my Google Ads not converting into jobs?

Spending money and getting clicks can feel like progress, but clicks are not jobs. A campaign can look busy and still book nothing if the path from click to scheduled appointment leaks at every step. The honest first question is not "should I spend more," it is "where is the money leaking."

In almost every underperforming account, the leak is not the ad itself. It is the tracking that hides what is working, the page the click lands on, the searches you are paying for, and the speed of your follow-up. Each of those is fixable without raising the budget.

## The real reasons your Google Ads aren't booking jobs

Here are the leaks we see most often, roughly in order of how much they cost.

- **No call tracking.** Most home service leads are phone calls. If you are not tracking calls from ads, you are flying blind and cannot tell which ads book jobs.
- **Clicks land on your homepage.** A homepage asks visitors to choose. A landing page asks for the job. Sending paid clicks to a general homepage leaks them.
- **Keywords are too broad.** Without tight match types, you pay for searches that will never book, like "free" or "DIY" or the wrong service entirely.
- **No negative keywords.** The fastest way to waste budget is paying for irrelevant searches you never excluded.
- **Slow lead response.** Even perfect ads fail if no one answers the call or follows up fast. The first to respond usually wins the job.

## Symptom, cause, and fix

Use this to diagnose your own account.

| Symptom | Likely cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Spending, no idea what works | No conversion or call tracking | Set up call and form tracking first |
| Clicks but no calls | Homepage, not a landing page | Send ads to a focused service page |
| Wrong kinds of inquiries | Broad keywords, no negatives | Tighten match types, add negative keywords |
| Leads come in, jobs do not | Slow or no follow-up | Respond in minutes, add nurture |
| Cheap clicks, expensive jobs | Optimizing the wrong metric | Track cost per booked job |

## Why cost per booked job is the only number that matters

It is easy to celebrate a low cost per click or a low cost per lead. Neither pays your bills. The metric that does is cost per booked job: what you actually pay in ad spend to win one scheduled job. You get it by carrying your cost per lead through your close rate.

A campaign with cheap clicks and a broken landing page can have a brutal cost per booked job, while a campaign with pricier clicks and a strong page and fast follow-up can be highly profitable. ## How do I fix Google Ads that aren't working?

Work the path from click to job in order.

1. **Set up tracking first.** Call tracking and conversion tracking, so you can see which ads and keywords actually produce jobs.
2. **Send clicks to a focused landing page.** One service, one clear offer, one obvious way to book.
3. **Tighten keywords and add negatives.** Pay only for the searches that match the work you want.
4. **Speed up follow-up.** Answer fast, and add automated follow-up so no lead goes cold.
5. **Then judge the budget.** Once the path converts, scale the spend. Scaling a leaky funnel just loses money faster.

## Why does spending more rarely fix Google Ads?

When ads are not booking jobs, the instinct is to raise the budget. It almost never works, and here is why: more budget on a leaky funnel just buys more of the same leaks. If your clicks land on a homepage that does not ask for the job, doubling the spend doubles the wasted clicks. If you cannot tell which keywords produce jobs, more money flows to the same blind spots. Budget amplifies whatever is already happening, good or bad.

The contractors who turn Google Ads around almost always do it by fixing the path from click to job first, then scaling. Spending more is the last lever to pull, not the first. Fix the leaks while the budget is small, prove the funnel converts, and then add money to a machine that actually books work.

## How does proper tracking change everything?

Tracking sounds like a technical detail, but it is the difference between running ads and guessing. Most home service leads arrive as phone calls, and if those calls are not tracked back to the ad and keyword that produced them, you are managing the account in the dark. You cannot cut what is wasting money or double down on what is working, because you cannot see either.

With call tracking and conversion tracking in place, the fog clears. You learn that two keywords produce most of your booked jobs while a dozen others drain budget and book nothing. You learn which ad and which landing page actually convert. Every decision that was a guess becomes a number. That is why tracking is the first fix, not an afterthought: until you can measure cost per booked job, every other change is a shot in the dark.

## What should a Google Ads landing page actually do?

A homepage and a landing page are built for different jobs, and sending paid clicks to the wrong one quietly kills campaigns. A homepage is a lobby. It introduces the whole business and invites visitors to explore. A landing page is a handshake for one specific job. It speaks to the exact service the ad promised, answers the obvious questions, and makes booking or calling take seconds.

When a homeowner clicks an ad for "emergency AC repair" and lands on a general homepage, they have to hunt for what they came for, and many leave. When they land on a page about emergency AC repair, with a clear phone number and a simple way to book, far more of them convert. The ad and the page should make a single, consistent promise, and the page should ask for the job plainly.

## How does speed to lead decide whether the spend pays off?

You can run perfect ads, with tight keywords and a strong landing page, and still book nothing if no one answers fast. Paid clicks are perishable. A homeowner with a problem is often contacting several companies at once, and the first to respond usually wins the job. Every minute that passes after a lead comes in lowers your odds.

This is why follow-up belongs in any conversation about ad performance. Make sure calls are answered during business hours and recovered after them, and add automated follow-up so a missed call or a form fill gets an instant response. The fastest way to raise the return on your ad spend is often not in the ad account at all. It is in how quickly a human or a system responds to the lead the ad just bought.

## Frequently asked questions

### Why am I getting clicks but no calls from Google Ads?

Usually because the clicks land on a page that does not ask for the job, like a general homepage, or because the searches are not well targeted. Send paid traffic to a focused landing page about the specific service, tighten your keywords, and make sure call tracking is in place so you can see what is really happening.

### How do I know if my Google Ads are actually working?

Track cost per booked job, not just clicks or leads. Set up call and conversion tracking so you can tie ad spend to scheduled jobs. If you cannot connect a dollar of spend to a booked job, you do not yet have enough tracking to judge the campaign.

### Should I send Google Ads traffic to my homepage?

No. A homepage is built to help visitors explore and choose. Paid clicks convert better on a focused landing page about the exact service the ad promised, with one clear way to book or call. Sending ads to the homepage is one of the most common reasons campaigns leak.

### Why is my cost per lead so high on Google Ads?

Broad keywords, missing negative keywords, weak landing pages, and untracked calls all inflate cost per lead. Tighten targeting, send clicks to a strong page, and track conversions. Also remember that a higher cost per lead can still be profitable if those leads close well, so measure cost per booked job.

### Are Google Ads worth it for contractors?

Yes, when the path from click to job is built correctly. Google Ads give you control over targeting and messaging that other channels do not. They fail when tracking, landing pages, keywords, or follow-up are neglected, and those are all fixable without increasing spend.

### What is the fastest fix for underperforming Google Ads?

Tracking. Until you can see which ads and keywords produce booked jobs, every other change is guesswork. Set up call and conversion tracking first, then fix landing pages and keywords based on what the data shows.

### How long should I run Google Ads before deciding they don't work?

Give a properly set up campaign at least 60 to 90 days before judging it, and make sure tracking is in place from day one so you are judging real data rather than impressions. The first few weeks are for gathering signal: which keywords convert, which landing pages work, and where leads stall. A campaign that "isn't working" after two weeks has usually not had time to optimize, or is missing the tracking needed to see what is happening. Fix the funnel, let it gather data, then decide based on cost per booked job.
