Are Angi and Thumbtack worth it for contractors? It depends on your business: they deliver leads fast, which helps newer or slower companies fill a schedule, but you pay for shared leads, compete on price, and never own the customer. For established home service businesses, they usually work best as a supplement, not as your main pipeline.

## TL;DR: Key Takeaways

- Angi and Thumbtack are lead marketplaces: you pay for leads or contacts, not for booked jobs.
- Most leads are sold to several contractors at once, so you compete on speed and price the moment they arrive.
- They can be worth it for filling gaps, testing a new service, or getting started, but the cost per booked job often rises as you grow.
- You do not own the customer, the review, or the channel. When you stop paying, the leads stop.
- The durable alternative is your own pipeline: Google Business Profile, AI search visibility, reviews, and ads you control.

## What are Angi and Thumbtack?

[Angi](https://www.angi.com) (formerly Angie's List and HomeAdvisor) and [Thumbtack](https://www.thumbtack.com) are online lead marketplaces that connect homeowners with local service providers. A homeowner describes a job, and the platform sells that lead, or the homeowner's contact details, to contractors who match. You are paying the platform for access to demand it aggregates, not for a finished, booked job.

Both are large and well known, which is part of the appeal. Homeowners trust the brand, search volume is high, and you can be receiving leads within days of signing up. That speed is real, and for some businesses it is genuinely useful.

## How do Angi and Thumbtack work, and what do they cost?

The core model is pay per lead. On Thumbtack you are typically charged when a homeowner contacts you or you reach out to a job. On Angi you pay for leads in your categories and service area. Pricing varies widely by trade, job type, and market, and a single lead can range from a few dollars to well over a hundred for high-value work.

The detail that matters most is this: the same lead is usually sold to multiple contractors. The platform's incentive is to sell each lead more than once, so you are often one of three, four, or more businesses racing to respond to the exact same homeowner. Speed and price become the whole game.

## The case for using Angi and Thumbtack

There are real situations where these platforms earn their fee.

- **You are new or have open capacity.** When your phone is not ringing yet, paid leads beat an empty schedule.
- **You want speed.** Setup is fast and leads start flowing quickly, with no long content or SEO ramp.
- **You are testing a new service or area.** They are a low-commitment way to gauge demand before you invest in your own marketing.
- **You can respond instantly.** If you answer within minutes every time, you can win a healthy share of shared leads.

## The case against relying on them

The same model that makes them fast also creates the problems that show up as you grow.

- **Shared leads, not exclusive ones.** You frequently pay to compete against several other contractors for one homeowner, which pushes everyone toward the lowest price.
- **You do not own the customer.** The relationship, and often the review, lives on the platform. Stop paying and the pipeline goes dark immediately.
- **Lead quality is uneven.** Tire-kickers, wrong-area requests, and duplicate contacts are common, and refund processes can be slow.
- **The cost per booked job can climb.** A cheap-looking cost per lead can become an expensive cost per actual job once you account for close rate and shared competition.
- **No compounding.** Money spent on marketplace leads buys this month's contacts. It does not build an asset that keeps producing.

## Angi vs Thumbtack vs owning your pipeline

Here is how the two marketplaces compare to building demand you control.

| Factor | Angi / Thumbtack | Your own pipeline |
|---|---|---|
| What you pay for | Leads or contacts | The asset (site, profile, reviews, ads) |
| Exclusivity | Usually shared with competitors | Yours alone |
| Who owns the customer | The platform | You |
| Speed to first lead | Days | Weeks to months |
| What happens when you stop paying | Leads stop immediately | The asset keeps working |
| Long-term cost per job | Often rises with scale | Often falls as it compounds |

The honest read: marketplaces are a faucet, your own pipeline is a well. A faucet is useful when you need water today. A well is what you want for the long run.

## Who should use Angi and Thumbtack?

They tend to fit best for newer businesses, contractors with open capacity, and anyone testing a new service line or market. If you can respond to every lead within minutes and you track your true cost per booked job, you can make them pay.

They tend to fit worst for established companies that already have a reputation and want to lower their cost per job over time. If you are doing $1M or more and still renting most of your leads, you are likely paying a premium for demand you could increasingly own. This is the same reason [AEO for contractors](https://www.vervemd.com/blog/what-is-aeo-for-contractors) matters: being named directly when a homeowner asks is the opposite of competing for a shared lead.

## What is the alternative to lead marketplaces?

The alternative is to build demand you own, so fewer of your jobs depend on a monthly lead bill. The core pieces are a complete Google Business Profile, visibility in AI search, a website that converts, a steady flow of reviews, and ads you run on your own accounts. For one trade's version of the AI-search piece, see [how to get your HVAC company recommended by ChatGPT](https://www.vervemd.com/blog/how-to-get-your-hvac-company-recommended-by-chatgpt).

This does not mean never using a marketplace. It means shifting the balance over time, so the marketplace becomes a supplement you can turn off, not the foundation you cannot.

Speed-to-lead ties it together. Whether a job comes from Angi, a Google search, or an AI answer, the contractor who responds first usually wins it. Owning your pipeline only pays off if every inquiry gets answered fast, which is why response systems matter as much as visibility.

## How to get more out of them if you do use them

- **Respond in minutes, every time.** With shared leads, the first credible reply usually wins.
- **Track cost per booked job, not cost per lead.** The only number that matters is what a real job costs you.
- **Tighten your targeting.** Limit categories and service area to the work you actually want.
- **Ask every won customer for a review on your own Google profile.** Turn a rented lead into an asset you keep.
- **Set a monthly cap.** Treat it as a controlled test, not an open tab.

## How to tell if Angi or Thumbtack is actually profitable for you

The headline cost per lead hides the number that matters. To know whether a marketplace is paying off, work it back to the cost of a real, booked job and compare it to your margin.

Walk through a simple example. Say leads cost you 30 dollars each, and one in five turns into a booked job. That is five leads, or 150 dollars, per job. If your average job nets 800 dollars in profit, paying 150 dollars to win it is a strong trade. But if leads are shared and your close rate is really one in ten, the same job now costs 300 dollars in lead spend, and a low-margin service call can stop making sense.

Three numbers decide it: your true close rate on marketplace leads, your average profit per job, and the all-in lead cost per booked job. Track those for 60 to 90 days before you judge the channel. Most contractors who quit Angi or Thumbtack in frustration never ran this math. Most who use them profitably watch it every month.

## Common mistakes contractors make on lead marketplaces

The platform is rarely the whole problem. These habits quietly turn a workable channel into a money pit.

- **Judging on cost per lead instead of cost per booked job.** A cheap lead that never closes is the most expensive kind.
- **Responding slowly.** With shared leads, a reply in an hour usually loses to a reply in five minutes.
- **Leaving targeting wide open.** Accepting every category and the whole metro fills your pipeline with jobs you do not want.
- **Never asking for the review.** Winning a job and not converting that customer into a Google review wastes the one lasting asset the lead could have produced.
- **Treating it as the whole strategy.** When a marketplace is your only source of leads, you have no leverage and no fallback the day pricing or lead quality changes.

## So, are Angi and Thumbtack worth it?

Are Angi and Thumbtack worth it? For the right business, at the right stage, used with discipline, yes. They are a fast faucet when you need leads now. But they are rented demand, not owned demand, and the contractors who win long term use them as a supplement while they build a pipeline they control. If most of your jobs still come from a monthly lead bill, the goal is not to quit cold. It is to shift the balance, month by month, until the marketplace is the supplement and your own pipeline is the foundation. That is the position every established contractor should be working toward.

## Frequently asked questions

### Are Angi and Thumbtack leads exclusive?

Usually not. Both platforms commonly sell the same lead to multiple contractors, so you are often competing with several other businesses to respond to the same homeowner. Some exclusive or appointment-style products exist, but the standard model is shared leads.

### How much do Angi and Thumbtack cost per lead?

It varies widely by trade, job type, and market. A lead can range from a few dollars to well over a hundred for high-value jobs. Because leads are often shared and not every lead books, the more useful figure to track is your cost per booked job, not the headline cost per lead.

### Is Thumbtack better than Angi?

Neither is universally better. They use similar pay-per-lead models, and results depend on your trade, market, response speed, and how you manage spend. Many contractors test both, measure cost per booked job on each, and keep whichever performs better for their specific service area.

### Can I get refunds for bad leads?

Sometimes. Both platforms have processes to dispute clearly invalid leads, such as wrong service area or spam. Approval is not guaranteed and can take time, so build some expected waste into your math rather than assuming every bad lead is refundable.

### Do Angi and Thumbtack hurt my brand or SEO?

They do not directly hurt your SEO, but relying on them means the customer relationship and often the review live on their platform instead of building your own brand and Google presence. Over time that can leave you dependent on a channel you do not control.

### What is a better long-term alternative to Angi and Thumbtack?

Building demand you own: a complete Google Business Profile, visibility in AI search, a website that converts, consistent reviews, and ads on your own accounts. These compound over time and keep working without a per-lead bill, which lead marketplaces never do.

### Should I quit Angi and Thumbtack if I already use them?

Usually not all at once. If a marketplace is currently a meaningful share of your booked jobs, cutting it cold leaves a hole. The smarter path is to keep it running while you build owned demand, then taper your marketplace spend as your own pipeline picks up the slack. Quit when you can replace the volume, not before.
