A good cost per lead by trade in 2026 runs roughly 40 to 65 dollars through Google Local Services Ads and 90 to 150 dollars through Google Ads search, depending on trade, market, and urgency. Ultimately, "good" is whatever lets you book a job at a profit, which is set by your close rate and job value, not the headline number.

## TL;DR: Key Takeaways

- Local Services Ads (LSA) are usually the cheapest paid leads: about 39 to 59 dollars per lead by trade ([Searchlight Digital, 2026](https://searchlightdigital.io/google-local-service-ads-cost-per-lead/)).
- Google Ads search leads run higher, averaging around 90 dollars and climbing past 150 for emergency and high-value work ([Hook Agency](https://hookagency.com/blog/google-ads-vs-local-service-ads-home-services/)).
- The average home services cost per click is about 7.85 dollars ([LocalIQ, 2025](https://localiq.com/blog/home-services-search-advertising-benchmarks/)).
- The number that actually matters is cost per booked job, not cost per lead. A cheap lead that never closes is the most expensive kind.
- Owned demand (Google Business Profile, AI search visibility, reviews) lowers your blended cost per lead over time because it does not charge per lead.

## What is cost per lead (CPL)?

Cost per lead is what you pay, on average, for one prospective customer to contact you or be handed to you. You calculate it by dividing total spend on a channel by the number of leads it produced. If you spend 2,000 dollars on Local Services Ads and get 40 leads, your cost per lead is 50 dollars.

It is a useful gauge of a channel's efficiency, but on its own it is misleading. A 30 dollar lead that never books is worse than a 120 dollar lead that turns into a 9,000 dollar install. That is why CPL is the start of the analysis, not the end.

Roofing sits higher and swings hard with storm cycles, so treat its range as the widest of the group.

## Cost per lead by channel: LSA vs Google Ads vs owned demand

Channel matters more than trade for what you pay. The same plumber can see a 50 dollar lead from one channel and a 140 dollar lead from another in the same week.

- **Google Local Services Ads:** typically the cheapest paid leads, often 40 to 65 dollars, and you only pay for leads, not clicks. Reported averages cluster around 60 dollars across industries ([Bullseye Internet](https://bullseyeinternet.com/show-me-the-money-decoding-google-local-service-ads-pricing/)).
- **Google Ads search:** higher cost per lead, around 90 dollars on average and well past 150 for emergencies, but more control over messaging and landing pages ([Hook Agency](https://hookagency.com/blog/google-ads-vs-local-service-ads-home-services/)).
- **Owned demand (SEO, AEO, Google Business Profile, reviews):** no per-lead charge. The cost is the work to build it, and the blended cost per lead falls as it compounds.

## Why does cost per lead vary so much?

Five factors move your CPL more than anything else:

1. **Urgency.** Emergency plumbing and HVAC cost more per lead because the intent, and the competition, is highest.
2. **Market.** A lead in a dense, competitive metro costs multiples of the same lead in a smaller market.
3. **Channel.** LSA, paid search, and organic each carry very different per-lead economics.
4. **Branded vs non-branded.** Leads from people searching your name are far cheaper than leads from people searching the service.
5. **Season.** Roofing after a storm and HVAC in a heat wave spike demand and price at the same time.

## Why cost per lead is the wrong number to optimize

The metric that should drive your decisions is cost per booked job, not cost per lead. To get it, divide your cost per lead by your close rate.

Work an example. Say HVAC leads cost you 50 dollars each and you close one in four. That is four leads, or 200 dollars, per booked job. If the average job nets 1,200 dollars in profit, 200 dollars to win it is excellent. Now say a different channel delivers 30 dollar leads but you only close one in ten. That is 300 dollars per booked job for the cheaper-looking lead. The 50 dollar lead was the better buy.

Cheap leads that do not close are the most expensive leads you can buy. Always carry CPL through to cost per booked job before you judge a channel.

## What is a good cost per lead for HVAC, plumbing, electrical, and roofing?

- **HVAC:** roughly 45 to 55 dollars on LSA and 100 to 150 on paid search. Strong if your close rate and ticket size are healthy, which they usually are for installs.
- **Plumbing:** about 50 to 60 dollars on LSA. Emergency work costs more per lead but closes fast and at a premium.
- **Electrical:** often the lowest of the group, near 35 to 45 dollars on LSA, partly because urgency is lower on average.
- **Roofing:** the highest and most volatile, from 75 dollars to well over 200 on paid search, driven by storm cycles and high job values that justify the spend.

## What is a blended cost per lead, and why does it matter?

Most owners look at the cost per lead of one channel at a time. The more useful number is your blended cost per lead: total marketing spend divided by total leads from every source, paid and organic together. It is the figure that actually reflects what a customer costs your business.

Blended CPL is where owned demand quietly wins. Paid channels hold a steady price per lead no matter how long you run them. But leads from your Google Business Profile, organic search, AI search, and reviews carry no per-lead fee, so every one of them pulls your blended cost per lead down. A business that gets half its leads from owned channels can run a blended CPL far below the paid benchmarks, even while its competitors pay full price for every lead. That gap compounds, and it is the whole reason building owned demand beats renting it.

## How to lower your cost per lead

You cannot control the auction, but you can lower your blended cost per lead by leaning less on paid channels over time.

- **Build owned demand.** A complete Google Business Profile, AI search visibility, and a steady review flow bring in leads with no per-lead fee. See [AEO for contractors](https://www.vervemd.com/blog/what-is-aeo-for-contractors) for the AI-search piece.
- **Win more branded searches.** The more people who know your name, the cheaper your leads, because branded clicks are far less expensive.
- **Respond in minutes.** Speed-to-lead raises your close rate, which lowers cost per booked job even if CPL stays flat.
- **Tighten targeting.** Cut categories and areas that produce leads you do not want.

## How much can you afford to pay per lead?

The benchmarks tell you what leads cost. They do not tell you what you can afford, and that number is often higher than owners assume. It comes from your own economics, not from an industry average.

Start from the job and work backward. Take your average profit per booked job and your close rate on a channel. If a booked job nets 1,200 dollars and you are willing to spend up to a quarter of that to win it, you can afford 300 dollars in lead spend per job. At a one-in-four close rate, that works out to 75 dollars per lead, comfortably above the Local Services Ads benchmark for most trades. A lead that looked expensive is suddenly well within budget.

This is why high-ticket trades can outbid everyone in the auction. A roofer or HVAC installer with a 9,000 dollar job and healthy margins can profitably pay far more per lead than a low-ticket handyman and still come out ahead. Know your own ceiling before you decide a benchmark is too high for you.

## Cost per lead vs cost per click vs cost per acquisition

Three metrics get used interchangeably, and they should not be. Each measures a different step, and the gaps between them are where money leaks.

- **Cost per click (CPC)** is what you pay each time someone clicks an ad. For home services it averages about 7.85 dollars ([LocalIQ, 2025](https://localiq.com/blog/home-services-search-advertising-benchmarks/)).
- **Cost per lead (CPL)** is what you pay per actual inquiry. It is higher than CPC because not every click becomes a lead.
- **Cost per acquisition (CPA)**, or cost per booked job, is what you pay for a customer who actually hires you. It is higher still because not every lead closes.

The chain runs from clicks to leads to booked jobs, and value leaks at every step. A strong CPC means nothing if your landing page does not turn clicks into leads. A strong CPL means nothing if your team does not turn leads into jobs. When you compare channels, compare them at the deepest level you can measure, ideally cost per booked job.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is a good cost per lead for HVAC?

In 2026, a good HVAC cost per lead is roughly 45 to 55 dollars through Google Local Services Ads and 100 to 150 dollars through Google Ads search. Whether that is "good" for you depends on your close rate and average job value. With healthy install tickets, even a 150 dollar lead can be very profitable.

### What is a good cost per lead for plumbing?

Plumbing leads typically run about 50 to 60 dollars on Local Services Ads, with emergency work costing more per lead. Because emergency plumbing closes quickly and at a premium, a higher cost per lead is often still worth it.

### Are Local Services Ads cheaper than Google Ads?

Usually, yes. Local Services Ads commonly cost 40 to 65 dollars per lead, while Google Ads search leads average around 90 dollars and climb higher for urgent jobs. LSA also charges per lead rather than per click, though you have less control over messaging.

### What is a good cost per lead for electrical?

Electrical is often the most affordable of the major trades, commonly around 35 to 45 dollars per lead on Local Services Ads, partly because average urgency is lower than plumbing or HVAC. As always, judge it against your close rate and job value rather than the raw number.

### What is a good cost per lead for roofing?

Roofing has the highest and most variable cost per lead, ranging from about 75 dollars to well over 200 on paid search. It spikes with storm cycles. Because roofing jobs carry high values, contractors can often absorb a higher cost per lead and still profit.

### How do I calculate cost per booked job?

Divide your cost per lead by your close rate. If leads cost 50 dollars and you close one in four, your cost per booked job is 200 dollars. Then compare that to your average profit per job to judge whether the channel is worth it.

### Is a lower cost per lead always better?

No. A low cost per lead means nothing if those leads do not close. A cheap lead with a poor close rate can cost more per booked job than an expensive lead with a strong one. Always optimize cost per booked job, not cost per lead.

### What is the cheapest source of home service leads?

Over time, owned demand is the cheapest: leads from your Google Business Profile, organic search, AI search, and reviews carry no per-lead fee. Paid channels deliver faster but always charge per lead, so the lowest blended cost comes from building demand you control.
