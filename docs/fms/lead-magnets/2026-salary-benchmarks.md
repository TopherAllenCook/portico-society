### Why the gap exists, and why it's narrowing

Three structural factors:

1. **Asset-size correlation.** Compensation in banking is more tightly correlated with institution size than with role complexity. A community bank CFO often manages comparable regulatory, treasury, and reporting workload to a regional bank counterpart on a fraction of the asset base, but earns proportionally less.
2. **Bonus structure.** Community bank bonus pools are typically smaller and more conservative than regional bank pools, compressing total compensation rather than base.
3. **Talent supply tightening.** Since 2022, regional banks have intensified recruiting of community bank finance talent. The narrowing gap reflects compensation increases at community banks specifically aimed at retention.

## Page 4 — Credit Unions, 2026 Drivers, and About FMS

### Credit Union Compensation Considerations

The credit union sector includes approximately **4,600 federally insured credit unions** holding **$2.3 trillion** in combined assets as of year-end 2024 (NCUA). Compensation patterns diverge from community banks in three meaningful ways:

1. **Lower bonus weighting.** Not-for-profit structure caps performance-based bonus pools. Credit union finance leaders typically see base salary as a larger share of total compensation.
2. **Stronger benefits packages.** Defined-contribution retirement matching, longer PTO accrual, and richer healthcare benefits offset some of the bonus gap, particularly at established credit unions over $1B in assets.
3. **Compressed range.** The spread between smallest and largest credit union compensation is narrower than the community bank equivalent, in part because credit union asset growth has historically been steadier than community bank consolidation.

### What's Driving 2026 Compensation Decisions

Four factors are shaping board-approved compensation budgets for community bank and credit union finance leaders this year:

1. **Rate environment volatility.** Treasury and ALCO functions have grown in strategic weight at sub-$10B institutions. Boards are paying premiums to finance leaders who demonstrate ALM and IRR competence.
2. **Regulatory complexity.** CECL implementation, evolving call report requirements, and ongoing examination intensity for institutions in the $1B-$10B range are creating defensible justifications for compensation increases at the Controller and Internal Audit levels.
3. **Talent retention pressure.** Voluntary turnover of finance leadership at community institutions has remained elevated since 2022. Retention has become the dominant rationale for above-COL increases.
4. **Continuing education and visible career paths.** FMS member surveys consistently identify continuing education access and clearly defined development paths as among the top three retention factors named by finance professionals at sub-$10B institutions, ranking above bonus structure and ahead of equity-equivalent compensation.
