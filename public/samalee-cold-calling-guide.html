Verve MD Cold Calling Guide: Home Services

Version 3.1. Hook → Story → Offer. A skeleton, not a recital. Memorize the beats, then say it in your own cadence.

THE GOLDEN RULEYou are the good tech, not the pushy salesman. A good tech never quotes a job before looking at the system. The more questions you ask, the more they trust you. They arrive at the solution themselves. You never tell them what they need.

0. HOW TO USE THIS

This is a skeleton, not a script you read. Memorize the beats and the branch logic, then say it your way. Read it out loud until the pauses feel natural and it sounds like you, not like a page. Tone beats words every time. If you sound like you are reading, you lose them in the first five seconds.

1. THE GOAL

Two ways to win on every good call. You never leave a good call empty-handed.

  1. Primary: book a 15-minute discovery call on Chris's calendar with the decision-maker.
  2. Backup that is almost as good: if you cannot book live, capture the six audit fields so the free audit fires and the automated email sequence books them for you over the next few weeks.

Ideally you get both: book the call, and still capture the audit so it lands in their inbox before you talk.

2. BEFORE YOU DIAL

  • Pull their website and Google Business Profile. Note the owner's name (it's usually right on the about page, or the company is named after them). In a shop this size the decision-maker is the owner, sometimes the office manager or a spouse who runs the books. Ask by name, not "whoever handles marketing."
  • Have the booking link ready to text the second they say yes: cal.com/christopher-cook-jfcxhu/verve-discovery.
  • Best dial windows: mid-morning around 9:30 to 11:30, after the trucks have rolled out, and early afternoon around 1:30 to 3:30. Avoid 7 to 9am dispatch rush and end of day when the office is closing out jobs.
  • Know who picks up: usually a CSR or dispatcher, sometimes the owner themselves at smaller shops. If the owner answers, skip the gatekeeper play and go straight to the hook.

3. GETTING TO A HUMAN

Trades answer their phones better than almost any industry, because a missed call is a missed job. Still:

  1. If there's a phone menu, take the "schedule service" or "request an estimate" option. That line is always staffed, and that person can transfer you or give you the owner's name.
  2. No menu, just an answering service? Ask when the office is staffed and call back then. Don't pitch an answering service; they can only take messages.
  3. If it dumps you into voicemail, hang up. Do not leave a message on the first two attempts. Mark it and try a different window. See the voicemail policy.

4. THE HOOK

React warmly to whatever they said when they picked up. Quick, two seconds. Not "how are you today." Just a real human beat, a first-name intro, then the question.

"Hey, that's awesome. I'm Sam." [half a beat] "I just wanna know, are you guys booked out solid right now, or could your crew handle a few more jobs."

Downward inflection on "jobs." Then shut up. Don't fill the silence. About half will say some version of "we could always take more work." You're in.

The warm beat is tiny: they say "doing alright," you say "good to hear it." That's the whole beat. Then the question.

If the owner picks up a cell and you can hear the job site behind him: "Hey, sounds like I caught you on a job. Twenty seconds and I'll let you go." Then go straight to the hook question. Owners respect that you noticed.

5. DISARM

Only after they answer. This line is about not running the business. It is never about not being able to do the work.

"Got it. So real quick, I'm not a plumber, I've never run a crew. But I'm pretty good at getting the right calls to companies like yours. I'd love to send work your way if it's a fit."

Swap "plumber" for "HVAC guy," "electrician," or "roofer" depending on who you're dialing.

6. DIAGNOSE

Ask questions. Let them name the pain. Never pitch during diagnosis. One question at a time, wait for each answer.

  1. "When a new customer calls in, where do they usually say they found you. Word of mouth, Google, the lead sites, a mix."
  2. "And of those, which one actually brings you the jobs you want. The good ones, not the tire kickers."
  3. "Is there a kind of job you wish you saw more of, like the bigger installs or repipes, but the phone just doesn't ring for them."
  4. "(only if they're still talking) When you've spent money on marketing before, what's been the most frustrating part."

7. THE MIRROR

They convince themselves. Only after this do you offer.

"Okay, so what I'm hearing is, you're getting [X] but not enough [Y], and the last time you tried [Z] it didn't really do much. Sound about right."

8. THE OFFER (double-point close)

Lead with the live booking. If they won't commit to a time, fall back to the audit. Try for both.

"Got it. Here's what I'd do. I work alongside Chris, he's the strategist who's been getting home service companies in this exact spot more of the right jobs. He's doing free 15-minute calls this week, no pitch, just walks through whether it'd be a fit. Worth grabbing a spot Thursday or Friday."

If yes, book live: "Awesome. I'm texting you the link right now. Grab Thursday 2pm or Friday 10am, whichever works."

The text you send, word for word: "Hey [Name], it's Sam, good talking just now. Here's Chris's calendar, grab whatever works: cal.com/christopher-cook-jfcxhu/verve-discovery" · Send it while you're still on the phone and confirm it landed before you hang up.

If they won't pick a time, go to the audit: "All good. Let me just run you a free audit that shows where your company shows up when homeowners search the new way, ChatGPT, Google, Perplexity. No cost. What's the best email and your website."

Either way, capture the six fields below. Hand them to Chris or drop them into the New Audit tool.

9. IF THEY ASK "WHAT DO YOU GUYS DO?"

Do not describe a service. Answer with the question. This is your strongest line. Use it any time, gatekeeper or decision-maker.

"Honestly, easiest way to show you: do you know if your company comes up when someone asks ChatGPT or Google for the best [plumber / HVAC company] in [city]." [pause] "Most don't, and that's where a real chunk of homeowners are starting their search now. I run a free check that shows you exactly where you land. Want me to run yours."

11. END-OF-CALL CAPTURE (six fields)

Before you hang up on any interested call, get these six. The first five are required for the audit to run. The sixth makes the report sharper.

  1. Company name
  2. Their name (first and last) and title
  3. Best email (read it back letter by letter if it's not obvious; one typo and the whole email sequence dies)
  4. Website URL
  5. City (primary service area)
  6. Trade: plumbing, HVAC, electrical, roofing, or a mix (if it's a mix, mark "other" in the New Audit tool)

Nice to have: their phone, and the one kind of job they wish they saw more of. That last one goes straight into the audit as their stated challenge.

12. WHAT HAPPENS AFTER YOU CAPTURE

Hand the six fields to Chris, or drop them straight into the New Audit tool. From there the system runs itself:

  • Fires the audit on their website.
  • Emails them an acknowledgement right away.
  • Delivers the full audit report when it's ready.
  • Runs a five-touch email sequence over about 45 days, each with a booking link.

This is the double-point: they can book from the link you texted live, or from any email in the sequence. The moment they book either way, the sequence automatically stops, so they never get double-messaged. You don't have to track any of it.

13. VOICEMAIL

  • Attempts 1 and 2: no voicemail. Voicemails from an unknown number rarely get returned, and they tip your hand before you ever get to talk.
  • Attempt 3 and beyond: optional 10-second curiosity voicemail, then immediately send the free-audit email so there's a concrete reason to call back.
  • Never pitch on a voicemail. Never leave the offer. The only job of the voicemail is to make a callback feel worth it.
"Hey [Name], it's Sam. Quick one about how your company shows up when homeowners search the new way, ChatGPT and Google. Not a sales thing, I'll shoot you a short email too. Talk soon."

Under 10 seconds.

14. CRITICAL TECHNIQUES

  • Downward inflection: end every sentence with tone moving down, not up. Upward inflection signals uncertainty and triggers resistance.
  • Pauses are weapons: after the opener question, after the disarm, after each diagnostic question. Silence makes them talk.
  • Never prescribe before they name the pain: jump to "I help with AI search" before they tell you what's broken and you're the tech quoting a job without looking at the system. They won't trust it.
  • The opener never names the company: "I'm Sam with Verve" flags it as a sales call in three seconds. First name only.
  • "I," not "we": "we" sounds like a company they have to evaluate.
  • Talk like they talk: jobs, calls, trucks, crews, booked out. Never "patients," "clients," or "growth strategy." If it wouldn't be said across a tailgate, don't say it.
  • Pin times, not "sometime": every callback gets a specific window ("tomorrow at 8:45"), and you call at that time. Vague callbacks are dead leads.
  • Rushed decision-maker, 20-second version: "Quick one and I'll let you go. Free audit, shows where your company comes up when homeowners ask ChatGPT or Google for the best [trade] in [city]. Best email and I'll send it." Capture beats a perfect call.
  • Graceful exit: if they say no twice, thank them and move on. "Got it, thanks for your time." Leaves the door open.

15. CALL LOGGING

After every call, log these. Three weeks of this tells you which opener works, which company size converts, and where people drop off.

  1. Company name and phone number
  2. Did you hit a phone menu or answering service, and how did you get past it
  3. Who you spoke to (name, role)
  4. Booked live? Yes or no. Audit captured? Yes or no.
  5. What they said the problem was. Chris leads with this on his call. The most important thing you write down.
